How Many Words Is a 1-Minute Speech?

June 6, 2026 · SoftEdit Tools

A 1-minute speech is approximately 120 to 150 words at an average speaking pace. At 130 words per minute — a widely used benchmark for conversational speech — one minute comes to exactly 130 words. Faster speakers may fit 160 words; slower or more deliberate speakers might use only 100. One minute of speaking is about half a page of double-spaced text.

Where 1-Minute Speeches Are Used

One minute is shorter than it sounds when you are standing in front of people. It appears in several specific contexts:

  • Elevator pitches: The original "elevator pitch" was designed for the length of a short elevator ride — roughly 30 to 60 seconds. A one-minute version is the slightly expanded standard for startup pitches, networking events, and job fairs.
  • Self-introductions: Many meetings, workshops, and networking events ask attendees for a one-minute introduction. This is enough time to state your name, role, and one interesting detail.
  • Toastmasters Table Topics: The Table Topics segment of Toastmasters asks speakers to respond impromptu to a question for one to two minutes. One minute is the minimum accepted length.
  • Interview answers: A strong answer to "Tell me about yourself" should run about 90 seconds to two minutes — but one minute is often cited as the target for keeping the interviewer engaged before handing the conversation back.
  • News media sound bites: Television news segments typically use 10- to 30-second clips, but the prepared quote or answer that journalists request from sources is usually designed to work as a one-minute response that can be clipped.

How to Write a 1-Minute Speech

At 130 words, there is room for exactly one idea — no more. A structure that works:

  • Hook (1 sentence, ~15 words): One surprising fact, a direct question, or a single vivid image. No long preambles.
  • Main point (2–3 sentences, ~50 words): State your single argument or message as directly as possible.
  • Evidence or example (2 sentences, ~45 words): One concrete detail that makes the argument real. A statistic, a specific example, or a brief story.
  • Call to action or close (1–2 sentences, ~20 words): What you want the audience to think, feel, or do. The final sentence should be memorable.

At this length, the biggest mistake is trying to cover more than one point. A one-minute speech that attempts three ideas will land none of them. Choosing one idea and saying it well is harder than it sounds — but that constraint is also what makes a great short speech memorable.

Practicing for One Minute

Most people underestimate how short one minute is and write too much. Write a draft of about 130 words, then time yourself reading it aloud at a normal pace. If you finish before the minute is up, your pace is faster than average and you can add a few words. If you run over, cut — do not speed up.

Recording yourself is the most effective way to check both timing and delivery. What feels slow in your head often sounds appropriately measured on playback, and what feels normal often sounds rushed. Most phones can record voice memos; a single practice run before a real delivery is worth more than reading the script ten times.
