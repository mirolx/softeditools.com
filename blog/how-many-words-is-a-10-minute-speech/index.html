How Many Words Is a 10-Minute Speech?

May 10, 2026 · SoftEdit Tools

A 10-minute speech contains roughly 1,200 to 1,500 words, depending on how fast you speak. The most commonly cited average speaking pace is around 130 words per minute, which puts a 10-minute speech at approximately 1,300 words. Conversational speakers tend to land between 120 and 150 words per minute; formal presenters often slow down to 110–120 to allow ideas to land.

Why Your Speaking Pace Varies

Most people speak faster when they are nervous or excited and slower when they are tired or carefully choosing words. In rehearsal, you might hit 140 words per minute; on stage in front of a large audience, you might drop to 115 without realizing it. This is one of the main reasons speeches run long — the delivery pace in the moment is almost always slower than during practice.

Pauses also consume more time than most speakers budget for. A deliberate pause of two to three seconds after a key point, multiplied across a 10-minute talk, can add a minute or more to your actual delivery time. If your speech consistently runs over the allotted time, shortening the script is more reliable than speeding up your delivery, which tends to make the content harder to follow.

How to Write for a 10-Minute Slot

If you have been given a 10-minute speaking slot, aim for a draft of around 1,200 words rather than 1,300. The extra buffer accounts for real-world slowdowns — audience reaction, natural pauses, and the slight deceleration that comes with being in front of people. Writing to the edge of your time limit almost always means running over it in practice.

Structure matters as much as word count. A 10-minute talk works best with a tight three-part structure: a brief opening that establishes the single idea you will argue, a middle section that develops it with evidence or examples, and a close that tells the audience what to do or think differently as a result. Trying to cover more than one or two central ideas in 10 minutes typically means covering none of them with enough depth to be memorable.

Checking Your Word Count

Once you have a draft, paste it into the word counter at SoftEdit Tools to get an instant count. Divide the number by your target speaking pace to estimate your delivery time. If you are unsure of your natural pace, record yourself reading the speech aloud and check the duration — most phones show recording length in real time.

It is also worth checking your word count after editing. It is easy to cut or add paragraphs and lose track of the running total. Keeping the word counter open in a separate tab as you revise makes it effortless to check at any point without losing your place in the document.

The Bottom Line

Plan for 1,200 words when writing a 10-minute speech. If you know you speak faster than average, target 1,400. Always leave a small buffer rather than writing to the exact limit — a speech that runs slightly short is almost always better received than one that runs over time.
